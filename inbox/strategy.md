## 2026-02-23 | Strategy: Workforce Readiness Program Strategic Direction

Workforce Readiness Program Strategic Direction

Context

During preparation for the Greenville staff meeting and leadership discussions
around training capacity and workforce development, the Workforce Readiness
(WFR) initiative was evaluated not just as a local program but as the
foundation for a scalable workforce development strategy.

Strategic Insight

The training organization faces long-term workforce supply challenges as
experienced field personnel retire and demand for gas turbine service
continues to grow. Workforce Readiness programs represent an opportunity to
build a structured early-career pipeline that aligns industry training
requirements with education partners.

Strategic Direction

Position Workforce Readiness as a scalable national model rather than a
localized pilot. The strategy is to build a repeatable framework that could
eventually expand across multiple regions while maintaining quality and
alignment with OFS training standards.

Leadership Considerations

Expansion must remain disciplined to ensure that program growth does not
outpace instructor capacity or facility availability. Long-term scalability will
require coordination between training delivery, workforce planning, and
external education partners.

## 2026-03-13 | Strategy: Project Leopard — Manufacturing Training Program Direction

Subject: Project Leopard — Manufacturing Training Program Direction
Signals

Discussion with Christian provided several leadership signals regarding the
direction of the Leopard generator manufacturing training program.

The conversation confirms that leadership views the current analysis as
constructive and aligned with expectations. The work is beginning to shift from
early exploration toward discussions around program feasibility, ownership,
and potential investment.

Akey point reinforced during the discussion was the importance of maintaining
aclear boundary between OFS Training and manufacturing operations. OFS
is expected to support workforce readiness and training architecture, but not
assume long-term ownership of generator manufacturing qualification or
production training. Manufacturing leadership intends to retain responsibility
for on-the-job training and final skill validation.

Another important clarification was that the program should represent a
standard manufacturing training model, not a site-specific program tied to
Pensacola. While Pensacola has the most immediate hiring demand,
leadership expects the curriculum to be structured around core generator
manufacturing competencies that could ultimately support multiple sites
including PEN, SCH, and WRO.

On-the-job training was also explicitly separated from the program scope. The
training academy concept remains focused on pre-production readiness, with
final qualification occurring within the manufacturing plants.

Overall tone of the discussion was positive. Leadership expressed appreciation
for the progress and interest in the next update as the architecture continues
to develop around Stator Stack and Generator Assembly training.

Leadership implication: maintain current direction while continuing to reinforce
the boundary between workforce readiness training and manufacturing
qualification.

Subject: Steam Training Lab Lifting Incident — Leadership Signals

A rigging incident occurred during a Steam training lab where a chain fall two-
block caused a component to drop approximately two inches. The component
stabilized immediately and no injuries occurred.

The RCA identified procedural compliance and instructor oversight as
contributing factors. Lift paperwork was not consistently reviewed before
execution, and instructor supervision during lifting operations was not always
structured due to competing instructional responsibilities.

This incident also mirrors root causes recently identified in a GT lifting issue,
suggesting that safety learnings are not always transferring effectively across
product line training programs.

Another factor identified was student fatigue and complacency near the end of
long training programs, which may increase risk during practical lab activities
where attention to detail is critical.

Corrective actions underway include requiring instructor review and approval
of lift forms prior to lifting activities, mandating instructor presence during
lifting operations, and reinforcing expectations through safety stand-downs
and toolbox talks.

Leadership awareness item: inconsistent lift documentation procedures could
present audit exposure if not corrected and standardized across programs.

Leadership implication: reinforce structured lift supervision and ensure cross-
program communication of safety learnings between Steam and Gas Turbine
training teams
