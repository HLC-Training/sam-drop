# Communications Protocol

**Scope:** Global. Applies to every project and every drafting context.
**Status:** Public mirror. The private vault copy is upstream. Edit there, regenerate here.
**Last revised:** 2026-07-24

---

## The default

Write plainly. Short paragraphs, contractions, ordinary sentences. A message should read like it was typed between meetings, because that is what it needs to look like.

Email does not get Smart Brevity. No bolded "Why it matters." No bulleted "The ask." No headers, no section labels, no scaffolding. Three points means three short paragraphs. The ask goes in a normal sentence, and the reader is trusted to find it.

Most work emails run under 150 words. When a draft goes long, the usual cause is explaining context the recipient already has. Cut that first.

Teams messages are shorter and looser than email. The scaffolding rules apply there too.

---

## Where structured formatting still applies

Formal leadership documents. Written reports. Anything built to be scanned rather than read start to finish. Board materials, quarterly summaries, formal proposals.

The test is whether the reader will scan it or read it. Scanned documents can carry structure. Read documents should not.

---

## The humanizer pass

Every communication draft runs through the humanizer skill before delivery. Non-negotiable.

Specific patterns to strip:

- Em dashes and en dashes. None. Use a period, comma, colon, or parentheses instead.
- Rule-of-three cadence. Two items or four are fine, three is a tell.
- "Not just X, it's Y" and similar negative parallelisms.
- Participle tails: "ensuring alignment," "highlighting the need," "reflecting our commitment."
- AI vocabulary: leverage, robust, key, crucial, align with, underscore, landscape, delve, showcase, testament, vibrant, enhance, foster, pivotal.
- Generic upbeat closers. End on the actual last point.
- Synonym cycling. Repeat the real noun when repeating it is clearer.
- Copula avoidance. "The deadline is Friday," not "the deadline represents a firm commitment."
- Signposting. Don't announce what you're about to say, say it.

Vary sentence length. Some short. Some longer, with a clause that takes its time. Even mid-length cadence across every sentence is one of the loudest tells there is.

---

## What not to do

Do not seed typos or errors to sound more human. The problem this protocol solves is writing that reads as templated and overlong, not writing that reads as too clean. Errors do not address that, and they carry real cost: a leader whose messages contain random mistakes reads as careless, and there is no controlling which message gets forwarded onward. The fix is structure and voice.

Do not strip personality in the name of sounding less like AI. Flat and voiceless is its own tell.

---

## After every draft

Give a short note on what changed and why. Keep the note out of the draft itself so the draft can be copied clean.

---

## Voice calibration

The humanizer skill supports matching against a writing sample. Where a `voice-sample.md` file is available, use it as the reference for all drafting. It should hold five or six messages written before AI drafting began.
